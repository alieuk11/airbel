Research & Innovation at the IRC

The Airbel Impact Lab is the IRC’s center for research and innovation, combining a focus on ethical exploration and trial-and-error with rigorous research capacity. We help build the world's understanding of what does and does not work when responding to conflict and crisis, and work closely with communities on life-changing solutions.

What is the Airbel Impact Lab?

Founded in 1933 at the call of Albert Einstein, the IRC is at work in 40 countries, helping people to survive, reclaim control of their future, and strengthen their communities.

But with over 70 million people displaced, the world is facing an unprecedented crisis. To address the evolving and growing nature of this crisis requires not just more aid, but better aid.

That’s why the IRC created the Airbel Impact Lab. With our unique combination of rigor, creativity, and multi-sectoral skills, we super-charge life-changing solutions with the potential for scale — with people and ethics at the center of all we do.

Airbel brings together multidisciplinary teams of experts drawn from inside and outside of the IRC - blending human-centered design, machine-learning, and behavioral insights with on-the-ground expertise - to have a meaningful impact in some of the most challenging places around the world.

Our Work

The Airbel Impact Lab works in more than 30 countries around the world. We are at the frontier of conducting rigorous evaluations in humanitarian settings. We rigorously evaluate existing solutions, make sure projects are good value for money, and inject evidence into decision-making.

Where best practice can get better, we work to adapt existing solutions, or to create brand new ones, that can fill gaps and get us closer to solving the world’s biggest challenges. Working alongside the communities IRC serves, we’re looking for breakthrough solutions that can create a paradigm shift in the field.

The IRC has conducted 39 of the roughly 150 high quality impact evaluations ever conducted with refugees or in conflict-affected contexts

We’re using a matching algorithm, developed by Stanford’s Immigration Policy Lab, to help identify where refugees will thrive. Studies show that this approach could lead to gains in refugee employment outcomes of around 40-70% on average.

Scaling up our ComPAS project has the potential to treat 13 million more children and save 850,000 lives every year.

Why "Airbel"?

Airbel is named for Varian Fry, who arrived in Marseilles in 1940 with $3,000 and a mission: to help people escape occupied France. There he established the Air-Bel Villa, a secret safe house that helped thousands of people escape to freedom.

Like all heroes of the early International Rescue Committee, Fry had no playbook or standard operating procedure to guide him. He learned by doing, applying ingenious methods, embracing inventive thinking, taking calculated risks, and building on success. This spirit has persisted for over 75 years and echoes throughout all of our programs worldwide.
